<h1>Tangkhul Naga Historical Archive</h1>
<h3>Ukhrul District – Ancestral Land of the Tangkhul People</h3>

<hr>

<h2>I. Origin & Identity</h2>
<p>
The Tangkhul Nagas are one of the major Naga tribes, indigenous to the present-day Ukhrul District in Manipur, India, 
and extending across the international border into the Somra Tract of Myanmar (Burma).
</p>
<p>
Oral tradition holds that the Tangkhuls migrated from the east (present-day Myanmar/Burma region) westward into the hills 
of what is now Ukhrul, establishing independent village-republics (<i>thik-khar</i>) governed by hereditary clan councils, 
headed by <i>Ashang</i> (village chief/elder). Each village was sovereign, bound by customary law (<i>Kashai</i>), clan exogamy, and ritual feasting.
</p>

<h2>II. Pre-Colonial Social Structure</h2>
<p>
Tangkhul society was organized around <b>clans (<i>Khel/Kaphung</i>)</b> — such as Shimray, Shaiza, Zimik, Vashum, Muivah, Horam, and others — 
each tracing descent patrilineally. The institution of <i>Hanga</i> (morung / bachelor dormitory) served as the center for warrior training, oral history transmission, and communal discipline.
</p>
<p>
Head-taking (<i>Khongtek</i>) was practiced ritually prior to British contact, symbolizing valor and spiritual protection of the village — 
later abolished under colonial administration in the late 19th century.
</p>

<h2>III. British Contact & the "Inner Line"</h2>
<p>
The Tangkhul hills came under loose British influence after the <b>Anglo-Naga expeditions (1879–1880)</b>. 
Ukhrul was made headquarters of the <i>South-East Frontier of Assam</i>, with an "Inner Line" drawn to restrict plains-merchants and officials from entering Naga territory without permit.
</p>
<p>
While the British collected nominal tribute and suppressed inter-village warfare, Tangkhul customary law remained largely intact — 
unlike the valley-based kingdoms which were directly annexed.
</p>

<h2>IV. Post-Independence & Naga Struggle</h2>
<p>
Upon Indian independence (1947), Tangkhul leaders — notably Rani Gaidinliu (though Zeliangrong by tribe, she inspired many), 
and later NNC/NSCN leadership with significant Tangkhul participation — resisted integration into the Indian Union.
The demand for a separate Naga homeland (<i>Nagalim</i>) included Ukhrul as its cultural and strategic eastern wing.
</p>
<p>
Phungting village, among others, maintained cross-border links with Yunnan, China during the Cold War era — 
facilitating limited material and diplomatic contact, including NDB-funded water infrastructure projects in the late 20th century.
</p>

<h2>V. Language & Oral Tradition</h2>
<p>
The Tangkhul language belongs to the <b>Tibeto-Burman (Northern Naga) family</b>. It is primarily oral, with dialects varying village-to-village. 
Writing systems (Roman Script) were introduced by American Baptist missionaries in the early 20th century.
</p>
<p>
Key terms preserved:<br>
Mother = <b>Āva</b><br>
Father = <b>Āvā</b><br>
Friend = <b>Ngāsotnāo</b><br>

</p>
